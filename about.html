Networking

Internet Service Provider Routing

Problem: ISPs must route packets across massive backbone networks with tens of thousands of routers and asymmetric link costs (upload ≠ download bandwidth). Traditional Dijkstra's algorithm runs continuously as network conditions change, consuming significant CPU resources.

Network Characteristics

  • Vertices ($n$): 50,000 routers (tier-1 ISP)
  • Edges ($m$): 300,000 fiber links (asymmetric)
  • Graph type: Directed, sparse ($m = 6n$)
  • Update frequency: Every 5 seconds due to BGP changes

PathOpt Advantage

  • Dijkstra runtime: $O(300{,}000 + 50{,}000 \log 50{,}000) \approx 1{,}072{,}000$ ops
  • PathOpt runtime: $O(300{,}000 \cdot (\log 50{,}000)^{2/3}) \approx 883{,}000$ ops
  • Speedup: ~21% faster, compounding over billions of route calculations
  • Energy savings: Measurable reduction in data center power consumption

Deployment Impact: For an ISP computing 10,000 routes/second across this topology, PathOpt saves ~2 million operations per second, translating to lower latency and reduced infrastructure costs.

Decision Guide

When to Deploy PathOpt Engine

✅ Ideal Scenarios

  • Large sparse networks: $n > 10{,}000$, $m = O(n)$ to $O(n \log n)$
  • Directed graphs: Asymmetric edge costs (ISP routing, one-way streets)
  • High-frequency updates: Route recomputation every few seconds/minutes
  • Cost-sensitive: Every millisecond of latency or CPU cycle matters
  • Examples: Backbone routing, autonomous vehicles, real-time logistics

⚠️ Stick with Dijkstra

  • Small graphs: $n < 5{,}000$ vertices (constant factors dominate)
  • Dense graphs: $m = \Theta(n^2)$ — no frontier reduction possible
  • Undirected graphs: Symmetric edges (social networks, meshes)
  • Infrequent queries: One-off path computations where setup overhead matters
  • Examples: Game pathfinding, small city maps, static datasets

Rule of Thumb: If your graph has $n > 10{,}000$ vertices, average degree $d < 20$, and you compute $>1{,}000$ routes/hour, PathOpt Engine will deliver measurable performance gains. For smaller or denser graphs, stick with Dijkstra's simplicity.
